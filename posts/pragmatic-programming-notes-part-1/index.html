Pragmatic Programming Notes (Part 1)

January 16, 2019 3-minute read
marthinpsrb
technology

  1. Within the overall structure of a project there is always room for individuality and craftsmanship. This is particularly true given the current state of software engineering. Our engineering may seem as archaic as the techniques used by medieval cathedral builders seem to today’s civil engineers, while our craftsmanship will still be honored. Software engineering technic will always change, but what were you done in the past it will still be honored.

  2. It’s continue process, continuously making many small improvements, Every day, work to refine the skills you have and to add new tools to your repertoire. You’ll start seeing results in a matter of days. Over the years, you’ll be amazed at how your experience has blossomed and your skills have grown.

  3. Pragmatic it’s an attitude a style, a philosophy of approaching problems and their solutions. Always think in large context, aware in a big picture

  4. Responsibility is something you actively agree to. You make a commitment to ensure that something is done right, but you don’t necessarily have direct control over every aspect of it, You have the right not to take on a responsibility for an impossible situation.

When you make a mistake (as we all do) or an error in judgment, 
admit it honestly and try to offer options.

Don’t blame all the problems on a vendor, a programming language,
management, or your coworkers.
  1. Provide Options, Don’t Make Lame Excuses, don’t tell your team, it’s cannot work, is late, or is broken. Explain what can be done. does code have to be thrown out?
Don’t be afraid to ask, or to admit that you need help.

  1. Software entropy. Entropy is a term from physics that refers to the amount of disorder in a system. When disorder increases in software, programmers call it software rot.

  2. Don’t ever ignore the small stuff failure, because it’s look like story of broken window. When you ignore/neglect one things, like one broken window and then the other window was broken you will always ignore it and will be big problem for you. You may be thinking that no one has the time to go around cleaning up all the broken glass of a project. If you continue to think like that, then you’d better plan on getting a dumpster, or moving to another neighborhood. Don’t let entropy win.

  3. Don’t leave broken windows (bad designs, wrong decisions, or poor code) unrepaired. Fix each one as soon as it is discovered.

  4. The real world just won’t let us produce much that’s truly perfect, particularly not bug-free software

  5. You can discipline yourself to write software that’s good enough—good enough for your users, for future maintainers, for your own peace of mind.
